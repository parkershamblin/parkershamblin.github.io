## Full Article Draft
I am Parker Shamblin, a Computer Science student at the University of South Florida, and I spend part of my time building computer vision projects with Python and OpenCV. This article shares the workflow I use for small, focused vision projects and how I document the results for my portfolio.

### Why computer vision
Computer vision is a great way to practice turning raw data into structured outputs. It combines programming fundamentals with real-world constraints like lighting, noise, and performance. I enjoy the problem-solving aspect and the fact that the results can be visual and easy to understand.

### My OpenCV pipeline
Most of my projects follow a simple pipeline:

- Input and preprocessing: resize, normalize, and reduce noise
- Feature or object detection: edges, contours, or learned models
- Postprocessing: filter results and format output
- Visualization: overlays and annotated images for review

Keeping the pipeline clear helps me isolate issues and explain the logic to others.

### How I document computer vision work
Computer vision projects are easier to evaluate when the steps are visible. I document:

- Sample inputs and outputs
- The exact preprocessing steps used
- The rationale for chosen parameters
- A short explanation of what worked and what I plan to improve

This keeps the focus on engineering decisions and provides a clear way to compare iterations.
